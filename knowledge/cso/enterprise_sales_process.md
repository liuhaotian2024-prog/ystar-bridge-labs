# Enterprise Sales Process — CSO Knowledge Base

## MEDDIC Qualification Framework

**Definition**: MEDDIC is a sales qualification methodology that ensures you're pursuing winnable deals with clear business value. Stands for Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion.

**When to use**: Every enterprise deal >$25K ARR. Required before moving prospect from "Qualified" to "Opportunity" stage.

**Step-by-step**:
1. Metrics: Quantify the pain. "How much does manual audit compliance cost you annually?" Document specific numbers.
2. Economic Buyer: Identify who controls budget. Not the same as your champion. Get name and title.
3. Decision Criteria: "What are the 3-5 technical/business requirements you'll use to evaluate solutions?" Write these down verbatim.
4. Decision Process: "Walk me through what happens after this call." Map: evaluation → legal review → procurement → signature.
5. Identify Pain: Current broken workflow. For Y*gov: "Can you prove to SEC what your AI agents did last quarter?"
6. Champion: Internal advocate who sells when you're not in the room. Test: "Would you present Y*gov to your VP on my behalf?"

**Common mistakes**:
- Confusing champion with economic buyer (champion wants you to win; economic buyer signs checks)
- Accepting vague pain ("compliance is hard" vs "we spent $400K on failed audit last year")
- Not documenting decision process timeline with specific dates
- Moving to proposal before all MEDDIC elements are locked

## Challenger Sale Methodology

**Definition**: Sales approach where you teach prospects something new about their business, tailor messaging to their specific situation, and take control of the conversation. Opposite of "relationship selling."

**When to use**: Complex B2B sales where customer doesn't know they have a problem. Perfect for Y*gov because most companies don't know AI agent governance is a regulatory risk.

**Step-by-step**:
1. Warm-up (5 min): Build credibility. "We work with 3 banks that faced SEC questions about AI audit trails."
2. Reframe (10 min): Teach them something. "Most compliance teams think AI is just another software vendor risk. But SEC treats AI agent actions like employee actions — you need complete audit chains."
3. Rational Drowning (15 min): Show cost of inaction. Use case studies, regulatory citations, financial impact.
4. Emotional Impact (5 min): "If SEC asks about your Claude Code agent's file access in a investigation, what would you show them?"
5. New Way (10 min): "Here's how our pharma clients handle this..." Introduce Y*gov as the solution.
6. Your Solution (10 min): Demo CIEU audit trail.

**Common mistakes**:
- Teaching generic information (use regulatory news specific to their industry)
- Being arrogant instead of insightful
- Not tailoring the "reframe" to their exact pain (financial vs pharma vs developer)
- Showing product too early (must earn the right by teaching first)

## Discovery Call Structure

**Definition**: First substantive conversation with qualified lead. Goal is disqualification OR advancement with MEDDIC complete.

**When to use**: After initial qualification (right company size, right industry, responded to outreach).

**Step-by-step** (45-minute call):
1. Re-set agenda (2 min): "I have three goals: understand your AI compliance workflow, determine if Y*gov fits, and if so, outline next steps. Does that work?"
2. Their story (10 min): "Walk me through how you're using AI agents today." Shut up and listen. Note: tools, frequency, what data they touch.
3. Pain quantification (10 min): "What happens when an auditor asks about AI-generated decisions?" → "How much time does your team spend on compliance documentation?" → "What's the cost if you get it wrong?"
4. Current solutions (5 min): "What are you doing today to solve this?" (Reveals competition and workarounds)
5. Decision context (8 min): MEDDIC questions. "Who else cares about this problem?" "What's your evaluation process?" "What's the timeline?"
6. Micro-demo (8 min): Show ONLY the feature that solves their top pain. For compliance officer: CIEU audit export. For developer: two-line install.
7. Clear next step (2 min): "Based on what you've shared, I propose a 30-day pilot with your compliance team. I'll send a proposal by Friday. Does that timeline work?"

**Common mistakes**:
- Talking more than 30% of the call
- Demoing features they don't care about
- Not asking about budget or timeline (afraid of being pushy)
- Ending call without scheduled next step
- Accepting "send me information" as a next step (you lost the deal)

## Proposal Writing

**Definition**: Written document that translates discovery insights into a clear business case for buying Y*gov.

**When to use**: After MEDDIC-qualified discovery. Never send a proposal before you know economic buyer, decision process, and quantified pain.

**Step-by-step**:
1. Executive Summary (0.5 page): "Based on our conversation on [date], Y*gov will reduce your AI compliance documentation time from 40 hours/month to 2 hours/month, saving $85K annually."
2. Current State (0.5 page): Mirror back their pain in their words. Quote them. "As [Name] mentioned, 'We have no way to prove to SEC what our trading algorithm accessed.'"
3. Proposed Solution (1 page): How Y*gov solves it. Include architecture diagram for technical buyers. Focus on outcomes, not features.
4. Implementation Plan (0.5 page): Week 1: Install. Week 2: Integrate with Claude Code. Week 3: First audit report. Week 4: Compliance team review.
5. Investment (0.5 page): Pricing table. Include ROI calculation using their numbers from discovery.
6. Next Steps (0.25 page): "Sign by [date] → kick off [date] → first audit report [date]."

**Common mistakes**:
- Generic proposals (must customize with their metrics, their language, their pain)
- Listing features instead of business outcomes
- Hiding pricing or making it complicated
- No timeline or clear next action
- Sending PDF (send Google Doc so you can see who's reading it)

## Negotiation Principles (Chris Voss Framework)

**Definition**: Tactical empathy-based negotiation. Treat objections as requests for help, not attacks.

**When to use**: Price objections, legal redlines, procurement pushback.

**Step-by-step**:
1. Label their emotion: "It sounds like the price is higher than you expected."
2. Tactical pause: Shut up. Let them explain.
3. Calibrated question: "How am I supposed to do that?" (when they ask for 50% discount). Forces them to solve your problem.
4. Accusation audit: Pre-empt objections. "You're probably thinking this is expensive for a governance tool."
5. "No"-oriented question: "Is now a bad time?" (easier to answer than "Is now a good time?")
6. Fair: "I want you to feel like you're treated fairly." (Most powerful word in negotiation)

**Common mistakes**:
- Defending your price (never say "but our value is...")
- Countering their offer immediately (use tactical pause)
- Accepting "I need to think about it" (use calibrated questions: "What about this doesn't work for you?")
- Discounting early (preserve discount for final close)

## Multi-Stakeholder Mapping

**Definition**: Identifying all people involved in buying decision and their motivations.

**When to use**: Every enterprise deal. Update after every call.

**Step-by-step**:
1. Draw org chart of all people you've met or heard mentioned
2. For each person, document:
   - Role/Title
   - What they care about (compliance officer: audit risk; CTO: engineering velocity)
   - Position on Y*gov (champion, neutral, blocker, unknown)
   - Influence level (high/medium/low)
3. Identify gaps: "Who's the economic buyer?" "Who's the legal approver?"
4. Plan access strategy: "Can [Champion] introduce me to [Economic Buyer]?"
5. Tailor messaging: Send different one-pagers to compliance (audit focus) vs engineering (developer experience focus)

**Common mistakes**:
- Only talking to your champion (they might not have power)
- Ignoring blockers (legal, security, procurement can kill deals)
- Same pitch to all stakeholders (tailor to their pain)
- Not asking "Who else should be involved in this conversation?"

## Closing Techniques

**Definition**: Tactical moves to convert verbal yes into signed contract.

**When to use**: After proposal delivery, verbal agreement, but contract hasn't moved.

**Step-by-step**:
1. Assumptive close: "Should we kick off on April 1 or April 8?" (assumes they're buying)
2. Puppy dog close: "Let's run a 2-week trial. If it doesn't work, walk away." (works for Y*gov — install is easy)
3. Summary close: "You said you need [X, Y, Z]. Y*gov delivers all three. What's left to decide?"
4. Silence close: After asking for the business, shut up. First person to speak loses.
5. Deadline close: "Our Q1 pricing expires Friday. After that, it's 20% higher." (only use if true)

**Common mistakes**:
- Not asking for the sale (afraid of rejection)
- Asking "What do you think?" instead of "Should we move forward?"
- Accepting "We'll get back to you" (always propose specific next action)
- Negotiating against yourself (offering discounts before they ask)

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before moving to proposal stage, confirm:
- [ ] MEDDIC complete (all 6 elements documented)
- [ ] Economic buyer identified and contacted
- [ ] Quantified pain ($X cost or Y hours wasted)
- [ ] Multi-stakeholder map created
- [ ] Decision timeline with specific dates
- [ ] Technical validation complete (if needed)
- [ ] Champion will advocate when you're not there

If any box is unchecked, do NOT send proposal. Do another discovery call.
