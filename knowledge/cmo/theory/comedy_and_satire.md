## Part 1: Four Major Comedy Theories

### 1. Benign Violation Theory (Peter McGraw) — PRIMARY FRAMEWORK

**Definition**:
Humor occurs when and only when three conditions are satisfied:
1. A situation is a **violation** (threatens one's beliefs about how the world should be)
2. The situation is **benign** (safe, acceptable)
3. Both perceptions occur **simultaneously**

**Key Mechanism**:
Three ways a violation becomes benign:
- **Alternative norms**: One interpretation is wrong, another is okay (like puns)
- **Commitment to violated norm**: The more distant you are from the norm, the funnier (e.g., men find sexist jokes funnier than women)
- **Psychological distance**: "Comedy is tragedy plus time"

**Application to "冒犯了，AI"**:

| Violation Element | Benign Element | Example Topic |
|------------------|----------------|---------------|
| CEO makes absurd AI claims | We know they're overselling, no real harm yet | "Sam Altman says AGI by 2027 (again)" |
| AI company burns billions | It's investor money, not ours | "Anthropic's $10B journey to build a polite chatbot" |
| Benchmark gaming reaches absurdity | Everyone in industry knows it's theater | "GPT-5 scores 99% on test it wrote itself" |
| Data labeling sweatshops | Workers are far away, we can critique without guilt | "Your AI ethics powered by $2/hour Kenyan workers" |

**Strategic Importance**:
- This is why we can "offend" safely — our targets are **powerful** (violation is against the mighty), and our critique is **constructive** (benign intent)
- The show name "冒犯了，AI" directly invokes this theory: we offend AI hype, not AI workers

### 2. Incongruity Theory — STRUCTURAL BACKBONE

**Definition**:
Humor arises from the perception of something **incongruous** — something that violates our mental patterns and expectations. The gap between expectation and reality is the source of laughter.

**Historical Lineage**:
- Origins: Aristotle (Rhetoric)
- Key philosophers: Immanuel Kant, Søren Kierkegaard, James Beattie (1779 — first to use "incongruous")
- Modern status: **Dominant theory** in contemporary humor research, most robust neuroscience support

**Neuroscience Evidence**:
Brain imaging (fMRI) shows activity in:
- Prefrontal cortex (detecting cognitive conflict)
- Temporoparietal junction (resolving conflict)
when a person "gets" a joke.

**Setup-Punchline Mechanism**:
- **Setup**: Creates expectation (normal mental pattern)
- **Punchline**: Violates that expectation (incongruity)
- Example: "I asked ChatGPT for relationship advice. [setup] It suggested I upgrade to GPT-5 for better emotional intelligence. [punchline — incongruity between 'relationship advice' and 'product upsell']"

**Application to "冒犯了，AI"**:

AI industry is **inherently incongruous**:
- **Promise vs Reality**: AGI next year vs autocomplete today
- **CEO narrative vs Engineer reality**: "Transforming humanity" vs "I fixed the tokenizer bug"
- **Marketing vs Truth**: "AI assistant" vs "if-else with statistics"
- **Valuation vs Revenue**: $100B valuation vs $0 profit

Our job: **expose and amplify these natural incongruities**.

**Episode Structure Template**:
1. Present CEO/company claim (setup — audience expects normal tech announcement)
2. Show what it actually is (punchline — incongruity revealed)
3. Heighten the absurdity (tag line — even more incongruous comparison)

### 3. Superiority Theory (Thomas Hobbes) — USE SPARINGLY

**Definition**:
Laughter is an expression of **"sudden glory"** arising from recognizing our superiority over others' infirmities, or our own past failings.

**Hobbes's Formula** (1651):
"The passion of laughter is nothing else but a sudden glory arising from sudden conception of some eminency in ourselves, by comparison with the infirmities of others, or with our own formerly."

**When Superiority Triggers Laughter**:
- Success beyond expectations
- Perception of others' defects
- Perception of our own past defects
- Conception of absurdity

**Why We Use This SPARINGLY**:

**Don't Mock**:
- Individual engineers (punching down)
- Small startups trying to innovate (punching down)
- Open source maintainers (punching down)
- AI safety researchers who care (punching laterally at allies)

**DO Mock**:
- CEO hubris (punching up)
- Venture capitalists' herd mentality (punching up)
- Big Tech's anticompetitive behavior (punching up)
- Our own industry's past mistakes (punching at self — Hobbes's "own formerly")

**Application Example**:
- BAD: "Look at this junior ML engineer who doesn't understand transformers" (punching down)
- GOOD: "Remember when we all thought blockchain would solve AI alignment?" (our own past absurdity)
- GOOD: "Elon Musk promised self-driving in 2016, 2017, 2018, 2019..." (punching up at repeated failure)

**Philosophical Criticism**:
18th-century critic Francis Hutcheson noted: we can feel superior to dogs, cats, trees without being amused. Superiority alone doesn't explain humor. Modern scholars mostly reject this as a **complete** theory, but it remains useful for understanding **some** types of humor (especially roasts).

### 4. Relief Theory (Sigmund Freud) — CONTEXTUAL USE

**Definition**:
Humor functions as a release of **psychological tension** or **nervous energy**, particularly tension built up around taboo topics (sex, aggression, death).

**Freud's Framework** (1905 — "Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious"):
- Jokes allow us to express **repressed desires** (especially aggression and sex)
- Humor is a **release valve** for socially unacceptable impulses
- Three categories: joke (Witz), comic, mimetic

**Spencer's Hydraulic Model**:
Laughter as release of pent-up nervous energy (precursor to Freud's psychological model).

**Modern Status**:
**Almost completely rejected** in contemporary philosophy and psychology. Today, "almost no scholar in philosophy or psychology explains laughter or humor as a process of releasing pent-up nervous energy."

**Why We Still Reference It**:
Even though the theory is outdated, the **observation** remains useful: humor often accompanies taboo topics.

**Application to "冒犯了，AI"**:

Industry Taboos We Can "Release":
1. **"The emperor has no clothes"** — Everyone knows LLMs are just autocomplete, but VCs keep funding as if it's magic
2. **"We're all complicit"** — Everyone in AI benefits from hype even as we mock it
3. **"It might actually be dangerous"** — The tension between "AGI is here!" marketing and "we have no idea what we're doing" reality
4. **"The money is fake"** — Billion-dollar valuations based on... vibes?

When we make these jokes, we're giving the audience **permission** to laugh at things they normally can't say out loud in their company Slack.

**Technique**: Build tension (discuss the taboo seriously for 10 seconds), then release with absurdity.

Example:
- "OpenAI says GPT-5 will cure cancer. [tension builds] Which is great, because GPT-4 gave my aunt cancer by suggesting essential oils for her tumor. [release]"

## Part 2: Stand-Up Comedy Techniques Toolkit

### 2.1 Setup-Punchline Structure (The Foundation)

**Basic Formula**:
- **Setup**: Establishes characters, situation, expectation
- **Punchline**: Conclusion that subverts expectation (almost always surprising)

**Anatomy of a Joke**:
```
Setup (creates normal assumption)
    ↓
Audience makes mental prediction
    ↓
Punchline (violates prediction)
    ↓
Laughter (surprise + benign violation + incongruity)
```

**Efficiency Rule**:
"Maximize laughs with brevity: concise setups and potent punchlines."

**If a joke falls flat**:
1. Is the setup too long? (Trim it)
2. Is the punchline worth the setup length? (Punch it up)
3. Did audience make the assumption you expected? (Clarify setup)

**Application to Video Format**:
In 60-second videos, we have ~20 seconds for setup, ~10 seconds for punchline, ~10 seconds for tag lines, ~20 seconds for visual payoff/callback.

### 2.2 Broken Assumptions (Modern Setup-Punchline)

**Structure**:
```
Setup → Audience makes assumption → Punchline flips it
```

**The "But Statement" Technique**:
"[Normal part of joke], but [weird/inappropriate part]"

**Example**:
- Normal: "AI will revolutionize healthcare"
- Assumption: It will help doctors
- But: "AI will revolutionize healthcare... by automating the denial of insurance claims 10x faster"

**Why This Works**:
Audience actively participates by making the assumption. When you flip it, they laugh at their own expectation.

**Application to AI Topics**:

| Setup | Assumed Direction | Actual Punchline |
|-------|------------------|------------------|
| "GPT-5 can pass the Turing test" | It's human-like | "...if the human is a confident idiot" |
| "We're committed to AI safety" | They have safety measures | "...which is why we fired the safety team" |
| "Our model is 99% accurate" | It works great | "...at generating 99% convincing bullshit" |

### 2.3 Misdirection (The Art of Deception)

**Definition**:
Leading the audience to expect one thing, then delivering something completely different.

**Mechanism**:
Uses **shared assumptions** to guide expectations, then flips them with the punchline.

**Misdirection vs Broken Assumption**:
- Broken assumption: Flip the logical conclusion
- Misdirection: Flip the **topic itself** (bait-and-switch)

**Example**:
"I was so moved by OpenAI's mission to benefit all of humanity... [audience thinks: emotional moment coming] ...that I bought their stock. Oh wait, I can't, because they're a NONPROFIT. [flips to cynicism about corporate structure]"

**Application Strategy**:
Use misdirection for **act breaks** — when transitioning between segments, misdirect to re-engage attention.

### 2.4 Callback (The Glue of Long-Form Comedy)

**Definition**:
Repeating or referencing a joke from earlier in the performance, creating a **layered humor experience**.

**Why Callbacks Work**:
- Reward attentive viewers
- Create a sense of "insider" community
- Build narrative cohesion
- Generate bigger laughs the second/third time (compounding humor)

**Callback Strategy for "冒犯了，AI"**:

**Within Episode (Micro-Callbacks)**:
- Establish a running gag in first 10 seconds
- Reference it at 30 seconds and 55 seconds
- Each callback should add new information (not just repeat)

**Across Episodes (Macro-Callbacks)**:
- Episode 1: Mock a CEO's specific phrase
- Episode 3: Reference that phrase when covering related topic
- Episode 6 (season finale): Montage of all the times that phrase proved wrong

**Example**:
- Ep 1: Elon says "Full Self-Driving next year" (mock it)
- Ep 2: Cover different topic, but insert "...unlike Full Self-Driving, which is still coming 'next year'" (callback)
- Ep 6: "And our #1 prediction for 2027: Full Self-Driving... next year!" (callback with compounding sarcasm)

**Technical Note**:
"Callbacks are repeating a joke verbatim, or paraphrasing a joke, that got a laugh earlier."

### 2.5 Act-Out (Physical Comedy in Video)

**Definition**:
The comedian acts out a scene, portraying multiple characters with different voices/physicality.

**Two Types**:
1. **Narrative act-out**: Telling a story by acting it out
2. **Character POV switch**: Jumping between different character perspectives

**Why This Matters for Digital Human Format**:

Our show uses digital humans (数字人), which means:
- We can change appearance instantly (different "characters")
- We can use split-screen for dialogue
- We can exaggerate facial expressions beyond human capability

**Application**:
Instead of describing what a CEO said, **show** the digital human transforming into that CEO and delivering the line in exaggerated manner.

**Example Scene**:
```
[Narrator POV - our digital host]
"Sam Altman announced GPT-5 at the dev conference."

[Transform to "Sam Altman" character - act-out]
[Wide-eyed, breathless] "This changes EVERYTHING. We're 90% to AGI."

[Back to Narrator]
"Meanwhile, an actual OpenAI engineer..."

[Transform to "engineer" character]
[Exhausted, coffee-stained shirt] "I've been debugging a JSON parsing error for six hours."
```

### 2.6 Rule of Three (The Comedy Pattern)

**Definition**:
A pattern where the first two items establish a norm, and the third breaks it for comedic effect.

**Why Three?**:
- One item: No pattern
- Two items: Pattern begins, but no subversion yet
- Three items: Pattern recognized AND broken

**Formula**:
```
Item 1: Normal
Item 2: Normal (confirms pattern)
Item 3: WEIRD (subverts pattern)
```

**Application Examples**:

**CEO Promises**:
"Sundar Pichai promised Bard would be accurate, helpful, and [audience expects another positive]... slightly less embarrassing than Google+ ."

**Product Features**:
"Our AI can write code, analyze data, and [expects another capability]... make your CEO think you need to be replaced."

**Industry Trends**:
"First they said AI would replace truck drivers. Then they said radiologists. Now they're saying... [expects another job]... AI ethicists. Full circle!"

**Timing**:
Save the funny/surprising element for the **third position**. The third item gets maximum punch because audience has locked into the pattern.

**Variation: Heightening**:
Instead of subverting on the third, make each item progressively more extreme (still follows pattern, but escalates absurdity).

### 2.7 Tag Lines (Extending the Laugh)

**Definition**:
An additional punchline **after** the main punchline, adding another layer of humor to the same joke.

**Purpose**:
- Extend laughter
- Add new perspective on the joke
- Milk the premise for more value

**Structure**:
```
Setup
    ↓
Punchline (laugh)
    ↓
Tag (bigger laugh, or different laugh)
    ↓
Optional: Tag 2 (compounding effect)
```

**Example**:
- Setup: "OpenAI says they're building AGI for all of humanity"
- Punchline: "Weird way to say 'Sam Altman and whoever gives us money'"
- Tag: "Also, since when does 'all of humanity' have a $20/month subscription tier?"
- Tag 2: "Pretty sure Maslow's hierarchy didn't include 'ChatGPT Plus'"

**Application Strategy**:
In 60-second format, use ONE tag per joke (not multiple). Save multi-tag sequences for climax moments.

**Sources**:

## Part 3: American Late-Night Satire Analysis

### 3.1 Last Week Tonight with John Oliver (HBO)

**Runtime**: ~30 minutes (with commercial-free HBO format)

**Format Structure**:
- **0-10 minutes**: Quick hits on recent news (multiple short segments)
- **10-30 minutes**: One main story — deep dive on a single topic

**Key Innovation**:
Main segment often covers topics **NOT in current news cycle** — allows for evergreen content and deep research.

**Satirical Approach**:
- Irony, hyperbole, black comedy
- Exaggerated scenarios to highlight logical inconsistencies
- Frames policy failures through absurd analogies

**Visual Design**:
- Panel in upper-left for photos/graphics as comedic aid
- Full-screen video clips from news/documentaries
- "Commercial break" elements (produced comedy bits outside studio)

**Research Rigor**:
"A rigorously researched comedy show" — starts with **hundreds of pages of research** per episode.

Process: Research → Outline → Script → Table read → Revisions → Filming

**Narrative Technique**:
- Start with news event (hook)
- Zoom out to systemic pattern (investigation)
- Use comedy to make the systemic absurdity visible (revelation)
- End with call-to-action or devastating summary (payoff)

**What We Borrow**:
- Deep research as comedy foundation
- Exaggerated analogies (e.g., "This is like if McDonald's promised to cure obesity")
- Visual graphics as comedy enhancers
- Evergreen topic selection (not just this week's drama)

**What We Adapt**:
- Shorter format (60 sec vs 30 min) — must compress the arc
- No "quick hits" section — every episode is ONE focused story
- Digital human instead of human host — allows for character transformation

### 3.2 The Daily Show (Comedy Central)

**Runtime**: 22 minutes (with commercial breaks)

**Format Structure** (Three-Act):
- **A-Block**: Opening monologue (headline-driven, 5-7 min)
- **B-Block**: Deep-dive or correspondent segment (8-10 min)
- **C-Block**: Guest interview (7-10 min)

**Satirical Innovation**:
"Discursive integration" — blends authoritative news discourse with entertainment talk show format.

**Correspondent System** (Key to Scalability):
Comedians act as "senior experts" in various fields, delivering field reports or in-studio segments using **reductio ad absurdum** (taking real-world logic to its absurd conclusion).

**Evolution**:
- Early era (Craig Kilborn): Mocked ordinary people
- Jon Stewart era: Shifted to mocking **powerful** (politicians, media, corporations)
- Current: Continues punching up

**Comedy Approach**:
Not just entertainment OR news — **both simultaneously**. Satirizes the news media itself while delivering actual information.

**What We Borrow**:
- Correspondent system (we can have different digital human "experts" for different topics)
- Reductio ad absurdum technique
- Media critique as meta-layer (we can mock AI media hype)

**What We Adapt**:
- No guest interview (not feasible in 60 sec)
- Compressed monologue (10 sec vs 5 min)
- Correspondent segment IS the whole episode (not a sub-segment)

### 3.3 Weekend Update (Saturday Night Live)

**Runtime**: ~10 minutes (within 90-minute SNL show)

**Format**:
Newscast parody with anchor desk, two anchors delivering satirical news headlines.

**Structure**:
- Anchor introduces topic
- Delivers scripted joke (deadpan)
- Sometimes includes "correspondent" guests (cast members playing characters)

**Satirical Approach**:
"Fake news" format — looks like actual news broadcast, but every headline has a punchline.

**Historical Significance**:
SNL's **longest-running sketch** (since first episode, Oct 11, 1975). Shaped landscape of comedic political satire on TV.

**Tone**:
Irreverent commentary on current events, politics, pop culture. Often more biting/cynical than regular SNL sketches.

**What We Borrow**:
- **Brevity**: 10 min is closer to our format than 30-min shows
- **Headline + Joke structure**: Efficient delivery
- **Deadpan delivery**: Digital human can do this well
- **Mix of politics + pop culture**: We'll mix AI industry + broader tech culture

**What We Adapt**:
- Ultra-compressed (60 sec vs 10 min) — 1-2 headlines max
- No live audience (but can simulate reaction with editing)
- Digital human can break the "anchor" character more freely

### 3.4 Patriot Act with Hasan Minhaj (Netflix, 2018-2020)

**Runtime**: 20-30 minutes per episode

**Format**:
Single-topic deep dive per episode (no guest interviews, no news roundup).

**Physical Setup** (CRITICAL INNOVATION):
- No desk, no couch (deliberately avoided "just another John Oliver")
- Host roams stage
- **Surrounded by large screens** showing graphics and video
- High-energy, physical performance

**Visual/Tech Integration**:
"Rich technology displays" — graphics are not just aids, they're central to storytelling.

**Content Range**:
From light (hypebeast culture, music streaming) to serious (systemic corruption, geopolitics).

**Tone**:
"Valedictorian's nerdy book report meets class clown's pop culture references."

**Approach to Complex Topics**:
- Use personal narrative to make abstract issues relatable
- High information density (like Oliver) but faster pacing
- Pop culture callbacks to break tension

**Cultural Positioning**:
Aimed at "New Brown America" — multicultural, tech-savvy, globally aware audience.

**What We Borrow**:
- **Tech-forward presentation**: Our digital human + graphics-heavy format aligns with this

- **Personal angle**: Even though it's satire, find the human/relatable entry point

**What We DON'T Borrow**:
- Length (we're 60 sec, not 20 min)
- Live audience energy (we're pre-produced)

**Why This Show Matters**:
It proved you can do SERIOUS deep-dive investigative comedy on tech/society topics and reach young audiences. The format itself was innovative (tech integration, no desk).

## Part 4: Tech Satire Landscape

### 4.1 Silicon Valley (HBO, 2014-2019)

**Genre**: Scripted sitcom (not talk show, but relevant for satire techniques)

**Runtime**: 30 minutes per episode

**Satirical Target**:
The entire Silicon Valley ecosystem — startups, VCs, tech giants, founder culture.

**Comedy Techniques**:
- **Sharp satire**: Real industry behaviors taken to logical extreme
- **Character-driven humor**: Archetypes (visionary CEO, anxious engineer, predatory VC)
- **Fast pacing**: 30 min is long enough for story, short enough to avoid dragging

**Key Satirical Patterns**:

| Industry Behavior | Silicon Valley Exaggeration |
|------------------|----------------------------|
| Pivot culture | Company pivots 5 times per season |
| Tech jargon | "Making the world a better place" repeated ad nauseam |
| VC pitches | Increasingly absurd valuations for increasingly trivial ideas |
| Bro culture | Toxic masculinity as core plot driver |

**What We Learn**:
- **Archetypes work**: Audience quickly understands "the Elon type" vs "the Sam Altman type"
- **Repetition amplifies satire**: When you show a pattern repeatedly, it becomes impossible to unsee
- **Authenticity matters**: Show was praised for technical accuracy (e.g., realistic coding problems)

**Application to AI Industry**:
We can create archetypal characters:
- "The AGI Prophet" (always predicting AGI next year)
- "The Safety Theater Performer" (talks about safety, ships anyway)
- "The Vibe-Based VC" (invests based on founder charisma, not tech)
- "The Exhausted Engineer" (just wants to fix bugs, gets pulled into keynote)

### 4.2 AI Comedy Satire Landscape (2026)

## Part 5: Short-Form Comedy for Digital Platforms

### 5.1 The 60-Second Challenge

**Platform Context**:
TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, LinkedIn video — all prioritize 15-60 second content.

**Attention Span Reality**:
"Average online attention span is about 8 seconds." **First 8 seconds determine scroll or stay.**

**Optimal Length Research**:
- Comedic sketches with tight pacing: 15-60 seconds
- Sweet spot for brand content: 15-60 seconds
- Our target: **55-60 seconds** (max out the window without losing pacing)

---

### 5.2 Structure for 60-Second Comedy

**The Hook (0-8 seconds)**:
CRITICAL. Must use one of:
- **Pattern interrupt**: Something visually/audibly unexpected
- **Direct promise**: "I'm going to tell you the real reason Sam Altman was fired"
- **Provocative question**: "Why is every AI demo faked?"
- **Bold statement**: "OpenAI just admitted they have no idea what they're doing"

**Hook Writing Rule**: Write the hook FIRST, not last. Everything else exists to deliver on the hook's promise.

**The Setup (8-25 seconds)**:
- Establish context (what happened?)
- Set expectation (what should this mean?)
- Keep it tight — every word must earn its place

**The Punchline (25-35 seconds)**:
- Subvert expectation
- Deliver the comedic insight
- Use visual aid if possible (graphics, video clip)

**The Tag/Escalation (35-50 seconds)**:
- Add additional layer
- Heighten the absurdity
- Connect to broader pattern (if macro-callback)

**The Outro/CTA (50-60 seconds)**:
- Callback to opening
- Subtle brand/show positioning
- "If this industry had governance frameworks..." (seed Y*gov concept without naming it)

---

### 5.3 Pacing and Rhythm for Short-Form

**Edit Frequency**:
Top-performing TikTok content averages **one cut every 2-3 seconds**.

**For Comedy**:
- Match cuts to comedic beats
- Audio beat, narrative beat, or action on screen
- Establish rhythm from first frame

**Rhythm Matching**:
- Entertainment content: **Varied pacing** with unexpected moments
- Our approach: Steady rhythm (2-3 sec cuts) BROKEN at punchline (hold for reaction)

**Example Edit Timeline**:
```
0-3s: Hook (quick cut)
3-6s: Visual context (quick cut)
6-9s: Setup narration (medium hold)
9-12s: Setup continues (quick cut)
12-18s: Build tension (longer hold)
18-20s: Punchline (CUT on the reveal)
20-25s: Reaction beat (hold for laugh)
25-28s: Tag line (quick cut)
28-35s: Visual escalation (rapid cuts)
35-40s: Callback (medium hold)
40-50s: Outro (slow down pacing)
50-60s: Final beat (hold on message)
```

---

### 5.4 Audio Strategy

**Audio is Primary on TikTok**:
"Audio is not an afterthought—it is often the primary creative driver."

**Our Audio Layers**:
1. **Voiceover** (digital human narration) — clear, punchy, energetic
2. **Music bed** (subtle, doesn't compete with VO)
3. **Sound effects** (emphasize punchlines, transitions)
4. **Silence** (strategic pauses before big reveals)

**Rhythm Sync**:
Match visual cuts to audio beats (music or speech rhythm).

**Accessibility**:
Always include captions — many viewers watch muted, and captions increase retention.

---

### 5.5 Platform-Specific Adaptations

**LinkedIn** (Professional audience):
- Slightly longer setup (audience expects substance)
- Less aggressive music
- End with thought-provoking question
- Horizontal format preferred

**Twitter/X** (Snark capital):
- Front-load the snark
- Assume audience is already cynical
- Shorter setup (they're impatient)
- Square format for feed

**TikTok/Reels** (Entertainment-first):
- Hook must be INSTANT
- Higher energy music
- More visual effects
- Vertical format

**YouTube Shorts**:
- Can be slightly longer (up to 60s is fine)
- More forgiving of educational content
- Square or vertical

**Our Distribution Strategy**:
1. Master cut (60 sec, horizontal)
2. LinkedIn version (50-60 sec, professional tone)
3. TikTok/Reels version (45-55 sec, high energy)
4. Twitter version (30-40 sec, pure snark extract)

## Part 6: Integration — How Theories Inform Practice

### 6.1 Theory Stack for Each Episode

Every episode should layer multiple theories:

**Foundation** (Benign Violation):
- Target powerful entities (violation is safe)
- Critique is constructive (benign)

**Structure** (Incongruity):
- AI hype vs reality (natural incongruity)
- Setup expectation → punchline subverts it

**Technique** (Setup-Punchline + Rule of Three + Callback):
- Efficient setups
- Three-part structures
- Reference previous episodes

**Tone** (Relief):
- Give audience permission to laugh at industry taboos

**Avoid** (Superiority):
- Never punch down
- Only mock the powerful or our own past errors

---

```

- Benign Violation setup: Identify powerful target
- Incongruity tease: Promise to reveal gap

- Establish what happened (news/event)
```

## Part 7: Counterfactual Simulations (Risk Planning)

### Scenario 1: First Episode Completion Rate < 15%

**Diagnosis**:
- Hook failed (didn't grab in first 8 seconds)
- OR pacing too slow (lost them mid-episode)
- OR topic too niche (target audience wrong)

**Immediate Action**:
1. A/B test different hooks for Episode 2 (3 variations)
2. Analyze drop-off point (YouTube Analytics)
3. Survey 10 target audience members: "When did you lose interest and why?"

**Systemic Fix**:
- Establish hook testing protocol: Every episode, test 3 hooks before production
- Mandatory "8-second audit": Does first 8 seconds deliver pattern interrupt + promise?

---

### Scenario 2: Viral Backlash from Company/Fanbase

**Example**: "We mocked [BigTech Co], their fans brigade our comments"

**Immediate Response**:
1. **Do NOT apologize** if critique was accurate
2. **Do engage** with thoughtful critics (shows we're not trolling)
3. **Do ignore** bad-faith attackers
4. **Do double-down** with receipts (if we're right, prove it)

**Content Response**:
- Next episode: Meta-commentary on the backlash itself
- "The most predictable thing about criticizing [Company] was [Company] fans proving our point"
- Use Superiority Theory carefully: Mock the behavior, not individual fans

**Systemic Fix**:
- Pre-episode risk assessment: "Which companies have aggressive fanbases?"
- Prepare response plan BEFORE publishing
- Have receipts ready (citations, links, video evidence)

---

### Scenario 3: Digital Human Uncanny Valley Problem

**Diagnosis**: Audience comments "The digital human is creepy/distracting"

**Immediate Mitigation**:
1. Lean into it: "Yes, I'm a digital human. Ironic, right? A fake person critiquing fake AI claims."
2. Adjust character design: Less realistic, more stylized (cartoon-ish reduces uncanny valley)
3. Use motion more — static digital humans feel robotic, movement adds life

**Technical Fix**:
- Test different rendering styles: realistic vs semi-realistic vs cartoon
- Survey audience: "Which version feels most engaging?"
- Iterate every 2 episodes based on feedback

**Strategic Pivot**:
If unfixable, pivot to:
- Voiceover + graphics (no digital human visible)
- Human host (but this defeats the meta-commentary)
