# CEO Organization Building Frameworks

## Dunbar's Number and Team Size

**Definition**: Humans can maintain ~150 stable relationships. Organizational layers form at 5, 15, 50, 150.

**Thresholds and Implications**:
| Size | Dynamics | Management Need |
|------|----------|-----------------|
| 5 | Everyone knows everything | None |
| 15 | Informal coordination works | Light structure |
| 50 | Need formal processes | Middle management |
| 150 | Need hierarchy | Executive layer |

**Application at Y* Bridge Labs**:
- Current: 5 agents + Board = within first Dunbar layer
- Coordination: Direct communication, no hierarchy needed
- Future: Plan structure changes before hitting next threshold

**Step-by-Step for Scaling**:
1. Track effective team size (include all regular collaborators)
2. At 12-15: Introduce team leads or sub-teams
3. At 40-50: Formalize communication protocols
4. At 120-150: Build management hierarchy

**Common Mistakes**:
- Adding management too early (overhead without benefit)
- Adding management too late (chaos at transitions)
- Counting headcount instead of coordination load

## Two-Pizza Teams

**Definition**: If a team can't be fed by two pizzas (~6-8 people), it's too big.

**When to Use**: Team structure decisions, project staffing.

**Two-Pizza Team Principles**:
1. **Single Ownership**: One team owns one thing end-to-end
2. **Minimal Dependencies**: Teams can ship without waiting
3. **Clear APIs**: Interfaces between teams are explicit
4. **Autonomous Decisions**: Team decides how to achieve goals

**Step-by-Step Team Design**:
1. Define the mission (what this team owns)
2. List required skills (product, engineering, etc.)
3. Staff to 4-6 people with those skills
4. Define interfaces with other teams
5. Set goals, not methods

**Y* Bridge Labs Structure**:
```
CEO Agent (coordination)
|-- CTO Team: Y*gov development
|-- CMO Team: Content and brand
|-- CSO Team: Sales and customers
|-- CFO Team: Finance and operations
```

**Common Mistakes**:
- Teams too large (diffusion of responsibility)
- Teams without end-to-end ownership (dependencies)
- Sharing people across too many teams

## OKR Methodology (Grove/Doerr)

**Definition**: Objectives (qualitative goals) with Key Results (measurable outcomes).

**OKR Anatomy**:
- **Objective**: Inspiring, qualitative, time-bound
- **Key Results**: 2-5 per Objective, measurable, challenging (70% target)

**Step-by-Step Implementation**:
1. **Company OKRs** (CEO + Board): 3-5 Objectives per quarter
2. **Department OKRs**: Align to company, add department-specific
3. **Weekly Check-ins**: Rate confidence (on track / at risk / off track)
4. **Quarterly Scoring**: 0.0-1.0 scale. 0.7 = success.
5. **Retrospective**: Why did we hit/miss? Update next quarter.

**OKR Template**:
```
Q2 2026 OKRs - Y* Bridge Labs

OBJECTIVE 1: Launch Y*gov to first paying customers
  KR1: 10 companies complete onboarding
  KR2: $50K ARR
  KR3: NPS > 40 from early adopters

OBJECTIVE 2: Establish thought leadership in AI governance
  KR1: 5 blog posts published, 10K total views
  KR2: Speaking slot at 1 major AI conference
  KR3: 3 enterprise inbound inquiries from content
```

**Common Mistakes**:
- Too many OKRs (max 5 Objectives)
- Key Results that are tasks, not outcomes
- Setting easy targets (0.7 should be challenging)
- Not reviewing weekly
- Linking OKRs to compensation (kills ambition)

## Hiring Frameworks

### A-Player Identification

**Definition**: A-players are top 10% performers in their role at your compensation level.

**Topgrading Interview Questions**:
1. What were you hired to do?
2. What accomplishments are you most proud of?
3. What were some low points?
4. Who was your boss and what will they say about you? (We will ask them)
5. Why did you leave?

**Red Flags**:
- Cannot name specific accomplishments with metrics
- Blames others for failures
- Boss references are vague or unavailable
- Pattern of short tenures without explanation

### Culture Fit vs. Culture Add

**Definition**:
- Culture Fit: Shares core values and working style
- Culture Add: Brings new perspectives that strengthen culture

**Assessment Framework**:
| Evaluate | Culture Fit | Culture Add |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Question | "Will they thrive here?" | "Will they make us better?" |
| Test | Values alignment | Diversity of thought |
| Risk | Homogeneity | Value drift |

**Step-by-Step**:
1. Define 3-5 non-negotiable values
2. Assess fit on those specifically
3. Separately assess: What new perspective do they bring?
4. Hire for values fit + perspective diversity

## Patrick Lencioni's 5 Dysfunctions

**Definition**: Five interrelated dysfunctions that prevent team effectiveness.

**The Pyramid (bottom to top)**:
1. **Absence of Trust** -> Cannot be vulnerable
2. **Fear of Conflict** -> Artificial harmony
3. **Lack of Commitment** -> Ambiguity, no buy-in
4. **Avoidance of Accountability** -> Low standards
5. **Inattention to Results** -> Status and ego over team

**Diagnostic Questions**:
- Trust: Do team members admit mistakes and weaknesses?
- Conflict: Do meetings have passionate debate?
- Commitment: Do people leave meetings aligned?
- Accountability: Do peers challenge each other?
- Results: Do people sacrifice personal goals for team goals?

**Step-by-Step Intervention**:
1. Start at the base (Trust). Trust exercises, vulnerability modeling.
2. Only after Trust: Encourage healthy conflict.
3. After debates: Ensure explicit commitment.
4. After commitment: Enable peer accountability.
5. Maintain focus on collective results.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Trying to fix higher levels without foundation
- CEO avoiding conflict (models dysfunction)
- Accountability without trust (becomes toxic)
